Lecture 1: Introduction to Artificial Intelligence

Course Objectives

  • Understand basic concepts of AI
  • Explore AI technologies and historical development
  • Examine application fields including healthcare, finance, and creative industries
  • Analyze ethical implications and future trends

Core Concepts

Definition of AI

  • Artificial: Human-created systems
  • Intelligence: Capacity to learn, reason, and adapt
  • AI: Machines simulating human cognitive functions

Key Terminology

  • Machine Learning (ML): Pattern recognition from data
  • Deep Learning: Multi-layered neural networks
  • Natural Language Processing (NLP): Language understanding/generation
  • Reinforcement Learning: Reward-based decision-making

Insights from Prominent Computer Scientists

  • “Can machines think?” — Alan Turing, 1950
  • “Making a machine behave in ways that would be called intelligent if a human were so behaving.” — John McCarthy et al., 1956
  • “AI is not just about making machines intelligent, but also about ensuring that intelligence is aligned with human values and goals.” — Stuart Russell, 2019

AI Development Timeline

    1950s: Turing Test conceptualized
  • 1980s: Machine Learning emerges
  • 2010s: Deep Learning revolution
  • 2020s: Generative AI proliferation (GPT, DALL-E)

AI Classification

Strong vs Weak AI

  • Strong AI: Hypothetical self-aware systems
  • Weak AI: Task-specific applications (e.g., chatbots)

Types of Intelligence

  • Linguistic-Verbal Intelligence: Strong language skills
  • Logical-Mathematical Intelligence: Problem-solving abilities
  • Visual-Spatial Intelligence: Ability to visualize objects
  • Interpersonal Intelligence: Understanding others
  • Intrapersonal Intelligence: Self-awareness
  • Existential Intelligence: Contemplating philosophical questions
  • Digital Intelligence: Competence in digital skills

AI Ecosystem

  • Core Elements: Data, Algorithms, Computing Power, Application Scenarios
  • Integration: AI combined with cloud computing, big data, and IoT

Application Fields

Industry Implementations

  • Smart Mining:
    • 24/7 robotic inspection systems
    • AI-powered belt monitoring
  • Healthcare: Diagnostic imaging analysis
  • Finance: Fraud detection algorithms

NLP Applications

  • Sentiment analysis in customer feedback
  • Generative text models (GPT-4)
  • Multilingual machine translation

Ethical Considerations

Major Concerns

  • Algorithmic Bias: Discrimination in automated systems
  • Job Displacement: 47% of US jobs at risk (Oxford study)
  • Privacy Risks: Data exploitation in surveillance systems

Privacy Protection

  • Federated Learning frameworks
  • Adversarial training techniques
  • GDPR-compliant data handling

Future Challenges

  • Legal status of AI-generated content
  • Control mechanisms for autonomous systems
  • AI alignment with human values

Debates Over AI

  • Concerns about reality perception with deepfakes
  • Ethical issues in high-stakes sectors
  • Job displacement fears due to automation
  • Privacy and data security challenges
  • Control over autonomous AI systems
  • Societal impacts of AI on relationships and governance

AI+ Consequences

  • Powerful AI leading to amazing results
  • Privacy leakage from personal data usage
  • Increased monitoring of individuals by AI systems
  • Uncontrollable AI leading to unexpected consequences

Industry Statistics

  • Computer Vision dominates Chinese AI market (34% share)
  • AI model accuracy surpasses humans in specific tasks:
    • Image classification: 99.2% vs 96% human
    • Speech recognition: 97.5% vs 94.17% human
